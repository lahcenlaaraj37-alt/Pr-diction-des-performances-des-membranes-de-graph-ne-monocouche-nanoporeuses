# PROJECT OVERVIEW
================================================================

This application predicts the performance of single-layer nanoporous graphene (SLNG) membranes
for seawater desalination using machine learning models trained on molecular dynamics (MD)
simulation data extracted from 12 peer-reviewed scientific publications.

The tool was designed to replace expensive and time-consuming molecular dynamics simulations
(which require supercomputers and days of computation) with an instant, free, web-based
prediction tool accessible to researchers worldwide.

The project was developed as a Master's final year project (PFE) in Analytical,
Environmental and Quality Sciences (Chemistry), focusing on water scarcity solutions
for arid and semi-arid regions, particularly North Africa and the Arabian Gulf.

The models predict two key membrane performance indicators:
1. Salt Rejection (%) — how well the membrane blocks salt ions
2. Water Flux (molecules/ns) — how much water passes through the membrane

The machine learning algorithm used is CatBoost (Categorical Boosting), which was
selected after systematic comparison with XGBoost, Random Forest, SVM, Decision Tree,
and Linear Regression on both cross-validation and train/test protocols.

# WATER SCARCITY CONTEXT
================================================================

Water scarcity is one of the most critical global challenges of the 21st century.
Only 2.5% of Earth's water is fresh water, and less than 1% is directly accessible.
Global water demand has increased sixfold during the 20th century.

According to UN-Water 2023, approximately 2.2 billion people lack access to safely
managed drinking water, and 4 billion people face severe water scarcity at least one
month per year.

The most affected regions include:
- Arabian Gulf countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman): less than
  100 m³ of renewable freshwater per person per year, far below the 500 m³ water
  poverty threshold. These countries depend on desalination for over 70% of their
  drinking water.
- North Africa (Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, Egypt): rapidly depleting groundwater
  and river resources combined with strong demographic growth.
- South Asia (India, Pakistan): high and worsening water stress.
- Southern Europe (Mediterranean): moderate to high water stress.

Seawater desalination is one of the most promising long-term solutions to water scarcity.
Average seawater salinity is 35 g/L (35,000 ppm), and the goal is to reduce total
dissolved solids (TDS) below 500 ppm for drinking water according to WHO standards.

# DESALINATION TECHNOLOGIES — COMPARISON
================================================================

Current desalination technologies fall into two main families:

THERMAL PROCESSES:
- Multi-Stage Flash (MSF): energy consumption 10–16 kWh/m³, robust but inefficient
- Multi-Effect Distillation (MED): energy consumption 5–8 kWh/m³, often coupled with
  power plants for co-generation
- Vapor Compression (VC): 7–12 kWh/m³, mechanical or thermal compression

MEMBRANE PROCESSES:
- Reverse Osmosis (RO): energy consumption 3–5 kWh/m³, dominates the global market
  (~65% of installed capacity), uses thin-film composite polyamide membranes
- Nanofiltration (NF): 2–4 kWh/m³, semi-selective filtration
- Electrodialysis (ED): 1–3 kWh/m³, ion migration under electric field

Reverse osmosis (RO) is the dominant technology but suffers from:
- Biofouling and mineral scaling
- Chlorine degradation of polyamide membranes
- Flux-selectivity trade-off limitation
- Energy consumption still too high for widespread deployment in developing countries

This is why next-generation membrane materials like graphene are being researched.

# GRAPHENE — STRUCTURE AND PROPERTIES
================================================================

Graphene is a two-dimensional material consisting of a single atomic plane of carbon
atoms arranged in a hexagonal honeycomb lattice with a C-C bond distance of 1.42 Å.

It was first isolated in 2004 by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov at the University
of Manchester using mechanical exfoliation (the scotch tape method). They received the
Nobel Prize in Physics in 2010 for this discovery.

Key properties of graphene:
- Young's modulus: ~1 TPa (approximately 5 times stronger than steel)
- Ultimate tensile strength: ~130 GPa (strongest known material)
- Thickness: ~0.335 nm (one atomic layer, 100,000 times thinner than a human hair)
- Thermal conductivity: 3000–5000 W/(m·K), about 10 times better than copper
- Carrier mobility: ~200,000 cm²/(V·s), ~100 times better than silicon
- Specific surface area: ~2630 m²/g
- Intrinsic impermeability: perfectly impermeable to all gases and liquids when unpunched
- Optical transmittance: ~97.7% (nearly transparent)

Graphene synthesis methods:
- Mechanical exfoliation: highest quality crystals, small quantities, research only
- Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD) on copper or nickel substrates: best method for
  large-scale high-quality single-layer graphene production
- Chemical exfoliation from graphite oxide (Hummers method): produces graphene oxide
  (GO) in large quantities, less crystalline quality

# SINGLE-LAYER NANOPOROUS GRAPHENE (SLNG) MEMBRANES
================================================================

Single-layer nanoporous graphene (SLNG) membranes represent the most promising
next-generation approach for seawater desalination.

The working principle is simple but powerful:
Nanometer-sized pores are created in a single graphene sheet. These pores allow
water molecules (kinetic diameter ~2.75 Å) to pass through while blocking dissolved
salt ions (hydrated Na⁺: ~7.2 Å, hydrated Cl⁻: ~6.6 Å).

Why SLNG membranes are potentially superior to polymer RO membranes:
- Atomic thickness (~0.335 nm) nearly eliminates hydraulic resistance
- Theoretically 2–3 orders of magnitude higher water flux than commercial RO membranes
- Theoretical salt rejection up to 100% for small enough pores
- No chemical degradation (unlike polyamide membranes)
- Extremely thin membrane = minimal pressure drop

THREE APPROACHES to graphene-based desalination exist:

1. Graphene-Polymer Hybrid Membranes:
   Graphene or GO integrated into polymer matrices (polyamide, polyethersulfone, PVDF).
   Improves flux by 20–100% compared to commercial RO membranes. Technology Readiness
   Level (TRL) 6–8, pilot applications exist.

2. Multilayer Graphene Oxide (GO) Membranes:
   Stacked GO sheets with controlled interlayer spacing (6–10 Å).
   Water transport through 2D nanochannels between layers. TRL 5–7, advanced prototypes
   exist including at the National Graphene Institute (Manchester, UK).

3. Single-Layer Nanoporous Graphene (SLNG) — THIS PROJECT:
   Single perforated graphene sheet with precisely sized nanopores. TRL 2–4, currently
   studied almost exclusively through molecular dynamics simulations. This is the focus
   of this project and this prediction tool.

Current challenges for SLNG membranes:
1. Computational barrier: MD simulations require supercomputers (100–10,000 CPU/GPU
   cores), take days to weeks, and require specialized expertise in LAMMPS, NAMD, or
   GROMACS software. This tool aims to solve this problem.
2. Manufacturing barrier: Perforating a graphene sheet the size of a football field
   with uniform nanopores invisible to the naked eye remains an engineering challenge,
   though ion bombardment and plasma etching techniques are advancing rapidly.

================================================================
# MEMBRANE INPUTS — DETAILED EXPLANATION
================================================================

The prediction model uses 7 input parameters. These are exactly the same parameters
used in molecular dynamics simulation software (LAMMPS, NAMD, GROMACS).

--- INPUT 1: GEOMETRY (Pore Shape) ---

Geometry refers to the shape of the nanopores created in the graphene membrane.
It is a categorical variable with 5 possible values in the dataset:

- Hexagonal: Most common geometry in the literature (69.2% of dataset). Pores arranged
  in a hexagonal pattern matching the graphene crystal structure. Provides good balance
  between flux and selectivity.

- Circular: Second most common geometry (27.3% of dataset). Circular pores are often
  used in MD simulations for their symmetry and ease of parameterization. Slightly
  different transport characteristics compared to hexagonal.

- Ozark: Less common geometry (1.3% of dataset). Derived from specific pore creation
  methods, named after specific computational studies.

- Triangular: Rare geometry (1.1% of dataset). Triangular pores have sharp corners
  that can influence ion exclusion differently from circular or hexagonal pores.

- Rhombic: Rare geometry (1.1% of dataset). Diamond-shaped pores, studied in
  comparative geometry analyses.

The geometry affects how water molecules and ions interact with the pore edges, the
steric exclusion of ions, and the overall transport efficiency. Hexagonal geometry
typically offers the best combination of flux and selectivity in the published literature.

--- INPUT 2: PORE AREA (Å²) ---

Pore area represents the cross-sectional area of a single nanopore in the graphene
membrane, measured in square Angstroms (Å²).

Dataset range: 0.58 Å² to 205.3 Å²
Dataset mean: 51.8 ± 40.8 Å²
Dataset median: 44.0 Å²
Dataset quartiles: Q1 = 23.1 Å², Q3 = 63.6 Å²

Physical meaning:
- 1 Å = 0.1 nanometer = 0.0000000001 meter
- A pore area of 24 Å² roughly corresponds to a circular pore of diameter ~5.5 Å
- Water molecule kinetic diameter: ~2.75 Å (area ≈ 5.9 Å²)
- Hydrated Na⁺ ion diameter: ~7.2 Å (area ≈ 40.7 Å²)
- Hydrated Cl⁻ ion diameter: ~6.6 Å (area ≈ 34.2 Å²)

Effect on performance:
- Pore area is the MOST INFLUENTIAL parameter on water flux.
- Larger pore area = much higher water flux (approximately linear relationship).
- Larger pore area = lower salt rejection (ions can pass through larger openings).
- For 100% salt rejection, pore area must be small enough to exclude hydrated ions.
- There is a critical threshold (~24–40 Å²) below which NaCl rejection approaches 100%.
- Above this threshold, salt rejection drops rapidly with increasing pore size.

The pore area directly determines whether a membrane is a desalination membrane
(high rejection, lower flux) or a water purification membrane (high flux, partial rejection).

--- INPUT 3: APPLIED PRESSURE (MPa) ---

Applied pressure is the transmembrane pressure driving water through the graphene
nanopores, measured in megapascals (MPa).

Dataset range: 1 MPa to 2500 MPa
Dataset mean: 239.5 ± 446.6 MPa
Dataset median: 100 MPa

Important note: In MD simulations, pressures of 100–2500 MPa are commonly used
because simulation boxes are extremely small (nanometer scale) and require high
pressure to observe statistically significant water transport within simulation
time scales (nanoseconds). Real industrial RO desalination operates at 5–8 MPa.
The model has learned the relationship between pressure and flux across the full
simulation pressure range.

Effect on performance:
- Higher pressure = significantly higher water flux.
- The relationship between pressure and flux is approximately linear for most pore
  sizes and chemistries.
- Pressure has a small positive effect on salt rejection because faster water transport
  dilutes the concentration polarization layer near the membrane.
- Very high pressures (>500 MPa in simulation) can cause structural stress on the
  graphene lattice, which is accounted for in the model through the training data.

Physical interpretation: Pressure overcomes the osmotic pressure of the feed solution
and provides the driving force for water to flow through the nanopores against the
osmotic gradient. For seawater (35,000 ppm NaCl), the osmotic pressure is approximately
27 bar = 2.7 MPa.

--- INPUT 4: FEED CONCENTRATION (ppm) ---

Feed concentration is the salt concentration in the water that enters the membrane
(the feed solution), measured in parts per million (ppm) of NaCl equivalent.

Dataset range: 0 ppm to 201,670 ppm
Dataset mean: 82,304 ± 56,678 ppm
Dataset median: 58,440 ppm

Reference concentrations for context:
- Drinking water (WHO): < 500 ppm TDS
- Brackish water: 1,000 – 10,000 ppm
- Seawater (standard): ~35,000 ppm (3.5% NaCl)
- Concentrated seawater: ~58,440 ppm (used in many MD simulations)
- Hypersaline brine: 100,000 – 201,670 ppm (used in extreme MD simulations)

Effect on performance:
- Higher salt concentration = slightly lower water flux (higher osmotic pressure
  opposing water transport).
- Higher salt concentration = slightly higher salt rejection percentage because the
  concentration gradient creates additional osmotic back-pressure on ions.
- Higher salt concentration increases the ion-pore interaction frequency, which can
  affect transport selectivity at the molecular level.
- The competition between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions for pore passage is concentration-dependent.

The feed concentration parameter allows the model to adapt predictions for different
water sources: brackish water wells, standard seawater, concentrated industrial brines.

--- INPUT 5: TEMPERATURE (°C) ---

Temperature is the operating temperature of the desalination system, measured in
degrees Celsius (°C).

Dataset range: 2°C to 77°C
Dataset mean: 32.9 ± 14.9°C
Dataset median: 27°C (most MD simulations run at ambient temperature)

Effect on performance:
- Higher temperature = higher water flux.
  Reason: Water viscosity decreases with temperature (less resistance to flow).
  Also, water molecule thermal kinetic energy increases, improving transport
  through nanopores.
- Higher temperature = slightly lower salt rejection.
  Reason: More energetic ions can overcome electrostatic or steric barriers more easily.
- The temperature effect on flux is significant but secondary to pore area and pressure.
- At 27°C (300K), water viscosity is approximately 0.85 mPa·s.
- At 77°C (350K), water viscosity drops to approximately 0.37 mPa·s — less than half.

In real seawater desalination plants, feed water temperature varies with season and
geography: 15–20°C in temperate regions, 28–35°C in tropical/Gulf regions.

--- INPUT 6: PORE CHEMISTRY (Functionalization) ---

Pore chemistry (also called functionalization) refers to the chemical groups attached
to the carbon atoms at the edges of the nanopores in the graphene membrane.
It is a categorical variable.

When pores are created in graphene (by ion bombardment, plasma etching, or oxidation),
the carbon atoms at the pore edges are chemically unsaturated and must be terminated
with functional groups.

Available values in the dataset (18 distinct chemistries):

PRISTINE / UNFUNCTIONALIZED:
- Pristine: Pore edges left without specific functionalization (143 samples, 30.8%).
  Carbon atoms at pore edges are in their natural state. Moderate flux and rejection.

HYDROGEN-BASED:
- H (Hydrogen-terminated): Pore edges terminated with hydrogen atoms (114 samples, 24.5%).
  Hydrophobic character. Good balance of flux and salt rejection. One of the most
  studied functionalizations in the literature (Cohen-Tanugi & Grossman 2012).

HYDROXYL-BASED:
- OH (Hydroxyl-terminated): Pore edges terminated with -OH groups (96 samples, 20.6%).
  Hydrophilic character. Higher water flux than H-terminated pores due to
  water-OH affinity. Slightly lower salt rejection for same pore size. Most favorable
  for maximizing water throughput.

SILICON-BASED:
- SiH2 (Silicon dihydride): Silicon atoms at pore edges with two hydrogen atoms
  (21 samples). Creates specific steric and electronic effects on ion transport.
- Si(OH)2 (Silicon dihydroxide): Silicon with two hydroxyl groups (21 samples).
  Combines silicon steric effects with hydroxyl hydrophilicity.
- Si: Pure silicon termination (6 samples). Creates rigid steric barrier.

NITROGEN-BASED (charged/polar):
- NH (Amino): -NH group at pore edges (18 samples). Slightly positive charge character.
  Can repel cations (Na⁺) through electrostatic interaction.
- N (Nitrogen): Nitrogen atom substitution at pore edges (7 samples). Creates strong
  electronic effects, modifies ion exclusion significantly.
- NO (Nitrogen-oxygen): -NO groups at pore edges (7 samples). Creates mixed
  steric and electrostatic effects.

FLUORINE-BASED:
- FH (Fluorine-hydrogen mixed): Alternating F and H at pore edges (12 samples).
  Fluorine creates strong electronegativity that repels anions (Cl⁻). Can improve
  salt rejection through electrostatic exclusion in addition to size exclusion.

ORGANIC:
- 2-Methyl, 4-Methyl: Methyl groups (-CH3) at pore edges. Create steric bulkiness
  that modifies pore effective diameter.
- 1-Ethyl, 2-Ethyl: Ethyl groups (-C2H5). Larger steric bulk than methyl.
- 4F4H, 8F8H: Mixed fluorine-hydrogen organic terminations.
- B (Boron): Boron termination, creates electron-deficient pore edges.
- COO⁻ (Carboxylate): Negatively charged group. Strong electrostatic repulsion
  of anions (Cl⁻), particularly effective at high pH.

Summary of chemistry effects:
- OH functionalization maximizes water flux (hydrophilic, attracts water)
- H functionalization offers the best studied flux-rejection balance
- Charged groups (N, NO, FH, COO⁻) improve ion rejection through electrostatics
- Bulky organic groups (methyl, ethyl) modify effective pore size sterically
- Si-based groups create unique steric profiles different from carbon-based edges

--- INPUT 7: POROSITY (%) ---

Porosity represents the percentage of the total membrane area that consists of
nanopores (empty space) rather than solid graphene.

Dataset range: 0.029% to 70.29%
Dataset mean: 9.5 ± 13.1%
Dataset median: 5.29%
Dataset quartiles: Q1 = 2.56%, Q3 = 10.0%

Physical meaning:
- Porosity = (total pore area / total membrane area) × 100
- A porosity of 1% means 1% of the graphene sheet is perforated with nanopores
- A porosity of 50% means half the membrane is pores — very high, approaching
  structural limits of the graphene sheet

Effect on performance:
- Higher porosity = proportionally higher water flux (more pores = more water passages)
- Higher porosity = slightly lower salt rejection for very high porosities because
  pore-to-pore interactions can create transport pathways for ions
- The relationship between porosity and flux is approximately linear at low porosities
- At very high porosities (>30%), the structural integrity of the graphene sheet
  is compromised and performance may deviate from the linear model
- Real-world achievable porosities using current fabrication techniques:
  typically 0.1–5% for ion bombardment, up to 15–20% for plasma etching

Porosity is a critical engineering parameter because it determines the membrane
productivity per unit area, which directly impacts the economic viability of the
desalination plant (capital cost per unit of water produced).

================================================================
# MEMBRANE OUTPUTS — DETAILED EXPLANATION
================================================================

--- OUTPUT 1: SALT REJECTION (%) ---

Salt rejection is the primary performance indicator of a desalination membrane.
It measures the percentage of salt ions that are blocked by the membrane and do not
pass through to the permeate (product water) side.

Formula: Salt Rejection (%) = (1 - C_permeate / C_feed) × 100

Where:
- C_permeate = salt concentration in the water that passed through the membrane
- C_feed = salt concentration in the feed water entering the membrane

Dataset statistics:
- Minimum: 1.29% (essentially no rejection — large pores)
- Maximum: 100% (perfect rejection — very small pores or charged pores)
- Mean: 87.13%
- Median: 97.14%
- Standard deviation: 21.0%

Interpretation guidelines:
- 100% salt rejection: Perfect — no ions pass through. Typical for pores smaller
  than the hydrated ion diameter with appropriate chemistry.
- >99.5% salt rejection: Excellent — comparable to commercial high-performance RO
  membranes (e.g., Dow FILMTEC SW30HR, Toray TM820).
- 95–99.5% salt rejection: Good — suitable for most drinking water applications
  if starting from standard seawater (product water TDS < 1750 ppm from 35,000 ppm feed).
- 80–95% salt rejection: Moderate — may require a second pass or post-treatment.
- <80% salt rejection: Poor — not suitable for drinking water production from seawater.

The dominant mechanisms of salt rejection in SLNG membranes are:
1. Steric exclusion: Hydrated ions are physically too large to pass through small pores
2. Electrostatic exclusion: Charged functional groups repel ions electrostatically
3. Hydrodynamic exclusion: At high water flux, fast water transport carries more
   water relative to ion diffusion

The model predicts this value directly as a percentage between 0 and 100.

--- OUTPUT 2: WATER FLUX (molecules/ns) ---

Water flux measures the transport rate of water molecules through the graphene
membrane, expressed in molecules per nanosecond (molecules/ns) as used in MD simulations.

Dataset statistics:
- Minimum: 0.01 molecules/ns (very small pores or very low pressure)
- Maximum: 2855.46 molecules/ns (large pores at high pressure)
- Mean: 129.93 molecules/ns
- Median: 48.50 molecules/ns
- Standard deviation: 278.55 molecules/ns

Note on units: The molecules/ns unit is specific to molecular dynamics simulations.
In industrial practice, flux is expressed in L/m²/h (LMH) or m³/m²/day.
For reference, commercial high-performance RO membranes achieve 15–40 L/m²/h at
55–70 bar. The MD simulation values in molecules/ns correspond to extremely small
membrane areas (few nm²), so conversion to industrial units requires knowing the
membrane area and pore density.

Why flux is predicted as log(flux+1):
The raw water flux values span a very wide range (0.01 to 2855 molecules/ns), creating
a highly right-skewed distribution that makes direct prediction very difficult for
machine learning algorithms. The log(flux+1) transformation (base-10 logarithm of
flux + 1) compresses this range to 0.004 – 3.456 and produces a near-normal distribution.

The model internally predicts log(flux+1), and the application converts back to real
flux automatically using: Flux = 10^(predicted value) - 1

The transformed variable log(flux+1) statistics:
- Minimum: 0.004
- Maximum: 3.456
- Mean: 1.651
- Median: 1.695
- Standard deviation: 0.686

High water flux is desirable because it means more water produced per unit membrane
area per unit time, which reduces the required membrane surface area and capital cost
of the desalination plant.

================================================================
# RELATIONSHIPS BETWEEN VARIABLES
================================================================

PORE AREA AND SALT REJECTION:
There is a strong inverse (negative) relationship between pore area and salt rejection.
Small pores (< 24 Å²) generally achieve close to 100% salt rejection.
As pore area increases beyond ~40 Å², salt rejection decreases sharply.
The exact threshold depends on the pore chemistry (functionalization).
OH and charged groups (FH, N, NO) can maintain higher rejection at larger pore sizes
compared to H-terminated or pristine pores.

PORE AREA AND WATER FLUX:
There is a strong positive (direct) relationship between pore area and water flux.
Flux increases approximately linearly with pore area for a given pressure and chemistry.
This creates a fundamental trade-off: larger pores = more water flow but less salt rejection.
This is the central challenge in desalination membrane design — balancing flux and selectivity.

PRESSURE AND WATER FLUX:
Higher applied pressure always increases water flux.
The relationship is approximately linear for a given pore size and chemistry.
Pressure does not significantly affect salt rejection in most cases (slight positive effect).
This is why industrial RO systems operate at high pressures (55–70 bar for seawater).

FUNCTIONALIZATION AND WATER FLUX:
OH-terminated pores have higher water flux than H-terminated pores of the same size.
This is because water molecules form hydrogen bonds with -OH groups at the pore edge,
facilitating water entry and transport through the pore (hydrophilic effect).
Conversely, hydrophobic functionalization (F-terminated) may initially resist wetting
but creates a smooth transport pathway once established.

FUNCTIONALIZATION AND SALT REJECTION:
Charged functional groups (NO, FH, COO⁻, N) can significantly improve salt rejection
beyond what size exclusion alone would predict.
Negatively charged groups repel Cl⁻ (like charges repel).
Positively charged groups repel Na⁺.
This electrostatic exclusion mechanism is particularly important for borderline pore sizes
where steric exclusion alone is insufficient for >99% rejection.

POROSITY AND WATER FLUX:
Water flux scales approximately proportionally with porosity.
Double the porosity = approximately double the water flux per unit membrane area.
This is because each pore contributes independently to total water transport.
At very high porosities (>30%), this linearity breaks down due to pore-pore interactions.

TEMPERATURE AND WATER FLUX:
Higher temperature increases water flux due to reduced water viscosity.
The relationship follows an Arrhenius-type behavior: flux ∝ exp(-Ea/RT).
Temperature effects are secondary compared to pore area and pressure effects.
A temperature increase from 27°C to 77°C typically increases flux by 30–60%.

CONCENTRATION AND SALT REJECTION:
Higher feed salt concentration slightly increases the percentage of salt rejection.
This is because higher concentration creates a stronger osmotic back-pressure on ions,
combined with increased electrostatic screening effects in concentrated solutions.
However, higher concentration also reduces absolute water flux due to increased osmotic pressure.

# THE MACHINE LEARNING MODEL
================================================================

--- MODEL OVERVIEW ---

Two separate CatBoost models were trained:
1. Salt Rejection Model: Predicts Salt Rejection (%) directly
2. Water Flux Model: Predicts log(flux+1), converted back to molecules/ns for display

Both models were trained on the same dataset of 465 rows extracted from 12 peer-reviewed
MD simulation studies on SLNG membranes.

--- ALGORITHM: CATBOOST (CATEGORICAL BOOSTING) ---

CatBoost is a gradient boosting algorithm developed by Yandex (Russia) in 2017.
It is specifically designed to handle categorical variables natively without requiring
manual encoding, which is a key advantage for this project given the Geometry and
Pore Chemistry inputs are categorical.

How gradient boosting works:
1. Start with a simple initial prediction (mean of target variable)
2. Calculate the residuals (errors) of this prediction
3. Train a decision tree to predict these residuals
4. Add this tree to the model with a small weight (learning rate = 0.05)
5. Recalculate residuals and repeat for 1000 iterations
6. Final prediction = sum of all 1000 tree predictions

CatBoost's key innovation (ordered boosting):
CatBoost uses a technique called "ordered boosting" which processes training samples
in a random order to prevent target leakage and overfitting, particularly important
for small datasets like this one (465 samples).

CatBoost handles categorical variables by:
- Building statistics about the relationship between each categorical value and the target
- Using a special "greedy target statistics" method that is resistant to overfitting
- This avoids the dimensionality explosion of one-hot encoding

--- CATBOOST HYPERPARAMETERS (EXACT CONFIGURATION) ---

For ANYONE wanting to reproduce these models, use EXACTLY these parameters:

SALT REJECTION MODEL (CatBoost):
- iterations (n_estimators): 1000
- learning_rate: 0.050
- depth (max_depth): 6
- l2_leaf_reg (lambda): 3
- loss_function: RMSE
- eval_metric: R2
- random_seed: fixed (replicable training enabled)
- cat_features: ['Geometry', 'Pore Chemistry (Functionalization)']
- train/test split: 80% training, 20% testing (random state fixed)

WATER FLUX MODEL (CatBoost):
- iterations (n_estimators): 1000
- learning_rate: 0.050
- depth (max_depth): 6
- l2_leaf_reg (lambda): 3
- loss_function: RMSE
- eval_metric: R2
- random_seed: fixed (replicable training enabled)
- cat_features: ['Geometry', 'Pore Chemistry (Functionalization)']
- target variable: log(flux+1) — NOT raw flux
- output conversion: flux = 10^(predicted_log_flux_plus_1) - 1
- train/test split: 80% training, 20% testing (random state fixed)

PREPROCESSING (same for both models — Orange Data Mining configuration):
- Numerical features: Standardize (mean=0, variance=1) — StandardScaler
- Categorical features: One feature per value (one-hot encoding in Orange)
  Note: CatBoost in Python handles categoricals natively — specify cat_features parameter
- Missing values: Average/Most frequent imputation (no missing values in final dataset)

# FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (FAQ)
================================================================

Q: What is salt rejection?
A: Salt rejection is the percentage of salt ions that the graphene membrane blocks from
   passing through. A salt rejection of 99% means that if you put 10,000 ppm salt water
   in, you get approximately 100 ppm salt water out. Higher is always better for
   desalination. Commercial RO membranes achieve 99.5–99.8%.

Q: What is water flux?
A: Water flux measures how much water passes through the membrane per unit time.
   In this model it is expressed in molecules per nanosecond (MD simulation units).
   Higher flux means more water produced per unit membrane area, which is economically
   desirable. However, flux and salt rejection are in trade-off: optimizing both
   simultaneously is the central challenge of membrane design.

Q: Why does the model use log(flux+1) instead of direct flux?
A: Because raw flux values in the dataset range from 0.01 to 2855 molecules/ns —
   a range of over 5 orders of magnitude. This extreme range makes direct machine
   learning prediction very inaccurate. The logarithmic transformation compresses
   this range to 0–3.5, making the distribution approximately normal and allowing
   CatBoost to learn accurate relationships. The app converts back to real flux
   automatically.

Q: What is the role of pressure?
A: Pressure is the driving force that pushes water through the nanopores against osmotic
   resistance. Higher pressure always increases water flux approximately linearly.
   In industrial RO, seawater desalination operates at 55–70 bar (5.5–7 MPa).
   In MD simulations, 100–500 MPa is commonly used to observe transport in tiny simulation
   boxes. The model has learned the pressure-flux relationship across the full training range.

Q: What is functionalization (pore chemistry)?
A: Functionalization refers to the chemical groups attached to the carbon atoms at the
   edges of the nanopores in the graphene sheet. When you punch a hole in graphene, the
   edge atoms become chemically reactive and bond with surrounding atoms or groups
   (H, OH, F, N, etc.). These groups dramatically change how water and salt ions
   interact with the pore, affecting both flux and selectivity.

Q: Which functionalization is best for desalination?
A: It depends on the objective:
   - For maximum water flux: OH (hydroxyl) functionalization is best — hydrophilic
     groups attract water molecules and accelerate transport.
   - For maximum salt rejection: Charged groups (N, NO, FH, COO⁻) are best — they
     add electrostatic ion exclusion on top of size exclusion.
   - For balanced performance: H (hydrogen) termination is the most studied and offers
     a reliable flux-rejection balance.

Q: What is porosity and why does it matter?
A: Porosity is the percentage of the graphene membrane area that consists of pores.
   A 5% porosity means 5% of the membrane is open space (nanopores).
   Higher porosity directly increases water flux (more pores = more water passages).
   Real membranes currently achieve 0.1–5% porosity with ion bombardment methods.
   Very high porosity (>30%) weakens the graphene sheet structurally.

Q: Why use graphene instead of conventional polymer membranes?
A: Three key advantages:
   1. Atomic thickness (~0.335 nm vs ~100 nm for polyamide) means near-zero hydraulic
      resistance, enabling theoretically 2–3 orders of magnitude higher water flux.
   2. Perfect crystalline structure eliminates the defects and variability of polymer
      membrane synthesis.
   3. Chemical stability — graphene does not degrade with chlorine (unlike polyamide
      RO membranes which are chlorine-sensitive and require careful pretreatment).

Q: Why is SLNG not yet used commercially?
A: Two main barriers remain:
   1. Manufacturing: Perforating a large graphene sheet (think football field scale)
      with billions of uniform nanopores of precise size and chemistry is extremely
      challenging. Current techniques (ion bombardment, plasma etching) are promising
      but not yet economically scalable.
   2. Computational: Understanding and optimizing SLNG performance requires expensive
      MD simulations on supercomputers. This prediction tool helps solve this barrier.

Q: How accurate is the model?
A: For the Salt Rejection model:
   - R² = 0.885 on 10-fold cross-validation (explains 88.5% of variance)
   - R² = 0.842 on held-out 20% test set (93 unseen samples)
   - RMSE = 7.0% salt rejection on test set
   - This means predictions are typically within ±7% of the actual MD simulation value
   The accuracy is sufficient for rapid screening of pore designs before running expensive
   full MD simulations.

Q: Can I use this tool to replace molecular dynamics simulations?
A: This tool is designed for rapid screening and exploration of parameter space,
   NOT as a complete replacement for MD simulations. Use it to:
   - Quickly identify promising pore geometries and chemistries
   - Understand trends and relationships between parameters
   - Reduce the number of expensive MD simulations needed
   - Make the research accessible to groups without HPC access
   For final validation of a specific design, full MD simulation remains the gold standard.

Q: What are the input units?
A: Pore Area: Angstroms squared (Å²) — 1 Å = 0.1 nm = 10⁻¹⁰ m
   Pressure: Megapascals (MPa) — 1 MPa = 10 bar = 145 psi
   Feed Concentration: Parts per million (ppm) — equivalent to mg/L for dilute solutions
   Temperature: Degrees Celsius (°C)
   Porosity: Percentage (%) of membrane area that is pores
   Geometry: Choose from Hexagonal, Circular, Ozark, Triangular, Rhombic
   Chemistry: Choose from 18 available functionalizations

Q: What does it mean when salt rejection is 100%?
A: 100% salt rejection means that in the MD simulation, no salt ions passed through the
   membrane during the entire simulation time. This is the ideal case and occurs when:
   - The pore is smaller than the hydrated ion diameter
   - AND/OR the pore chemistry provides strong electrostatic repulsion of ions
   In practice (industrial scale), achieving and maintaining 100% rejection continuously
   is not realistic, but values >99.5% are achievable with well-designed SLNG membranes.

Q: What is the difference between water flux in molecules/ns and L/m²/h?
A: The molecules/ns unit is used in MD simulations because simulation boxes are
   nanometer-sized. To convert to industrial units (L/m²/h), you would need:
   - The actual number of pores per unit membrane area (determined by porosity and pore area)
   - The simulation box dimensions
   The model provides MD-scale flux for direct comparison with published MD studies.
   For industrial design, multiply by the pore density to get membrane-scale flux.

Q: How was the data collected?
A: Through systematic review of 50 scientific articles, from which 12 were selected
   as compatible with the project's data schema. Numerical data was extracted from
   published figures using PlotDigitizer software and from tables directly.
   The final dataset contains 465 rows with zero missing values and no artificial
   data augmentation — all data comes from real MD simulation results.

Q: Which molecular dynamics software generates this type of data?
A: The published data was generated using:
   - LAMMPS (Large-scale Atomic/Massively Parallel Simulator, Sandia National Labs, USA)
   - NAMD (Nanoscale Molecular Dynamics, UIUC, USA)
   - GROMACS (University of Uppsala, Sweden)
   All three require HPC clusters or supercomputers. A typical simulation uses
   100–10,000 CPU cores and takes 1–14 days of computation.

# GLOSSARY OF TECHNICAL TERMS
================================================================

Angstrom (Å): Unit of length equal to 0.1 nanometer (10⁻¹⁰ m). Used to measure
atomic distances. The graphene C-C bond is 1.42 Å.

CatBoost: Gradient boosting machine learning algorithm by Yandex. Handles categorical
variables natively. Selected as the prediction model for this project.

Cross-validation (CV): Model evaluation technique that splits data into K folds,
trains on K-1 folds and tests on 1 fold, rotating K times. 10-fold CV used here.

Desalination: Process of removing salt and dissolved minerals from seawater or
brackish water to produce fresh water suitable for human consumption.

Functionalization: Chemical modification of graphene pore edges with specific
chemical groups (H, OH, F, N, etc.) that alter transport properties.

Gradient Boosting: Machine learning ensemble technique that builds models sequentially,
each new model correcting the errors of the previous one.

Graphene: Two-dimensional material of single-layer carbon atoms in hexagonal lattice.
Discovered 2004. Nobel Prize Physics 2010.

log(flux+1): Base-10 logarithm of (water flux + 1). Applied to handle the extreme
range of flux values in the dataset. Model predicts this, then converts back.

Molecular Dynamics (MD): Computational simulation method that calculates atomic
trajectories by solving Newton's equations of motion. Used to simulate water and
ion transport through graphene nanopores.

Nanopore: A nanometer-scale hole in the graphene membrane. In this study, pores
range from 0.58 Å² to 205.3 Å² in area.

Osmotic pressure: Natural pressure difference across a semipermeable membrane due
to salt concentration difference. Seawater osmotic pressure ≈ 27 bar (2.7 MPa).

Porosity: Fraction of total membrane area consisting of nanopores, expressed as %.

R² (R-squared): Statistical measure of model accuracy. R²=1 means perfect prediction,
R²=0 means the model is no better than predicting the mean. Model achieves R²=0.885.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): AI technique where a language model retrieves
relevant information from a knowledge base before generating responses. Used by the
chatbot in this application.

RMSE (Root Mean Square Error): Square root of the average squared prediction error.
For salt rejection model: RMSE = 7.03% on test set.

Salt Rejection (%): Percentage of salt ions blocked by the membrane. Key performance
indicator. Formula: SR = (1 - C_permeate/C_feed) × 100%.

SLNG (Single-Layer Nanoporous Graphene): The specific type of graphene membrane
studied in this project — a single atomic layer with precisely sized nanopores.

TDS (Total Dissolved Solids): Total concentration of dissolved substances in water,
measured in ppm or mg/L. WHO drinking water standard: < 500 ppm.

Water Flux (molecules/ns): Rate of water molecule transport through the membrane
in molecular dynamics simulation units. Higher = more water production.

XGBoost: Extreme Gradient Boosting, another gradient boosting algorithm. Ranked
second (after CatBoost) in performance for this project.

# SCIENTIFIC CONTEXT — WHY THIS PROJECT MATTERS
================================================================

Access to clean water is recognized as a fundamental human right by the United Nations
(Resolution A/RES/64/292, 2010). Yet billions of people lack this basic necessity.

Traditional desalination (thermal processes) is too energy-intensive (10–16 kWh/m³)
for wide deployment in developing countries. Current RO membranes (3–5 kWh/m³) are better
but still require significant infrastructure investment.

SLNG membranes theoretically offer:
- 2–3 orders of magnitude higher water flux than polymer RO (less membrane area needed)
- Energy consumption potentially approaching the thermodynamic minimum (~1 kWh/m³)
- No chemical degradation (unlike chlorine-sensitive polyamide)
- Potentially lower capital and operating costs at industrial scale

The barrier to progress is computational: every new pore design must be tested by
expensive MD simulations on supercomputers that are only available to well-funded
research groups in developed countries.

This project democratizes SLNG membrane research by providing:
1. A freely accessible web tool (no registration, no cost, no download required)
2. Instant predictions (milliseconds vs days of HPC computation)
3. Universal access (works on any device, any country, any internet connection)
4. Scientific rigor (trained on real published MD data, not synthetic data)

The tool is designed for:
- University researchers in developing countries without HPC access
- Industrial engineers evaluating SLNG membrane potential
- Students learning about membrane science and machine learning
- Anyone curious about the physics of nanoscale water transport
